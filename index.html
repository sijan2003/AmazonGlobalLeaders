The Journey

1994
Founded

Amazon was founded by Jeff Bezos in his garage in Bellevue, Washington, initially as an online bookstore called Cadabra. It was renamed Amazon after the world's largest river, symbolizing the company's ambition to become the largest bookstore on Earth.

2006
AWS Launch

Amazon Web Services (AWS) was launched, providing cloud computing services. It started with simple storage and compute services but grew to become the leading cloud platform, powering a significant portion of the internet.

2015
Cloud Leader

By 2015, AWS had established itself as the market leader in cloud computing, surpassing competitors with its vast array of services and global infrastructure, contributing significantly to Amazon's profitability.

2026
AI Revolution

Amazon leads the AI revolution with advancements in generative AI, integrating it into AWS, Alexa, and logistics, further enhancing personalization and efficiency across its ecosystem.

Leadership

Jeff Bezos

Founder & Chairman

Jeff Bezos founded Amazon in 1994 as an online bookstore. Under his leadership, it grew into a global empire. He pioneered customer obsession, emphasizing long-term thinking over short-term profits, and invested heavily in innovation like AWS. He stepped down as CEO in 2021 but continues as Executive Chairman, influencing strategy.

Andy Jassy

CEO

Andy Jassy joined Amazon in 1997 and founded AWS in 2006, growing it into a multi-billion-dollar business. As CEO since 2021, he focuses on operational excellence, cost optimization, and expanding AI capabilities across Amazon's segments while maintaining the customer-centric culture.

Philosophy

  • Always Day 1 mentality: This approach keeps the company agile and innovative, as if it's still a startup, preventing bureaucracy and encouraging constant reinvention to stay ahead of competitors.
  • Long-term over short-term gains: Amazon prioritizes investments that may not pay off immediately but build sustainable advantages, like developing new technologies or entering new markets.
  • Invent on behalf of customers: The company focuses on solving customer problems before they even recognize them, leading to groundbreaking products like Kindle and Alexa.
  • Embrace being misunderstood: Bold innovations often face skepticism, but Amazon persists if convinced it's right for customers, as with the initial launch of AWS.

Leadership Principles

Customer Obsession

Leaders start with the customer and work backwards.

Explanation: This principle means that every decision at Amazon begins with understanding what customers need or want, even if they haven't expressed it yet. It drives innovation by focusing on customer pain points and delighting them with solutions.

Example: In meetings, an empty chair represents the customer, reminding participants to consider the customer's perspective in all discussions.

Invent & Simplify

Seek new ideas and innovate constantly.

Explanation: Amazon encourages employees to invent new ways to serve customers and simplify complex processes, fostering a culture of continuous improvement and efficiency.

Example: The 1-Click Ordering patent simplified the checkout process, reducing friction and increasing conversion rates in e-commerce.

Think Big

Bold vision creates results; thinking small is limiting.

Explanation: Leaders are encouraged to set ambitious goals that inspire teams and lead to breakthrough innovations, rather than incremental changes.

Example: Launching AWS when Amazon was primarily known as a bookstore transformed the company into a tech powerhouse.

Deliver Results

Focus on key inputs and deliver with quality.

Explanation: This emphasizes accountability, ensuring that teams not only set goals but also execute them effectively, measuring success by outcomes.

Example: Amazon's commitment to fast delivery, often meeting or exceeding promised timelines through optimized logistics and technology.
